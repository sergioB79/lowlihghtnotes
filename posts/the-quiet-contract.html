The Quiet Contract

To begin in not knowing, to end in not knowing, and to think in between as if it might still matter.

Even worse than the fear of death is the not knowing.

Not knowing how much of the world will remain closed to me. Not knowing the answers to questions that feel older than language itself. Not knowing what all this — this brief flare of consciousness — is actually for.

Ignorance, in a strange way, is the only honest place to begin. Every thought, every theory, every system starts there: in the quiet admission that we do not understand.

But the real unease comes later.

Because the more you live, the more you realise the scale of it. How vast the unanswered things are. How many doors will remain closed, not because you didn't try, but because there simply isn't enough time inside a human life.

And so you walk through the years gathering fragments. A few ideas. A few truths. A few illusions that felt convincing at the time. Yet the horizon barely moves.

In the end, you understand a little more than you did at the beginning.

But not nearly enough to change the fact that most of existence will remain unknown to you.

And perhaps that is the quiet contract of being alive: to begin in not knowing, to end in not knowing, and to think in between as if it might still matter.
